The Impact

How Curry Reshaped Every Basketball Mind on Earth

In the 2009 NCAA tournament, a skinny Davidson kid took the nation hostage with a performance so electric that scouts — who had labeled him too small, too slight, too fragile — were forced to watch film in quiet, humiliated disbelief. He scored 40 against Gonzaga. 30 against Georgetown. And then Davidson fell by two points to Kansas, ending perhaps the greatest tournament run by a single player in history.

When Curry entered the NBA Draft in 2009, he was selected 7th overall by the Golden State Warriors — a franchise that had been moribund for over a decade. What happened next is not merely a sports story. It is a cultural one.

The Three-Point Revolution

Teams once treated the three-pointer as a secondary weapon. A desperation shot. A play for specialists and role players. Curry turned it into the primary currency of basketball excellence. By the 2015–16 season, every team in the league was scrambling to find their own Curry. None could.

  • Three-point attempts league-wide increased by over 75% in the decade following Curry's rise
  • The midrange jumper — once a staple — became statistically endangered nearly extinct
  • Davidson College saw record application increases after Curry's tournament run
  • Youth basketball programs globally restructured to teach shooting mechanics first
  • The NBA all-star weekend's 3-point contest became the most-watched event
  • Multiple FIBA rule changes were influenced by analytics born from Curry's game

The Unprecedented MVP Season

In 2016, Stephen Curry became the first — and thus far only — player in NBA history to win the Most Valuable Player award unanimously. Every single voter. All 131 of them. Not one ballot went to any other player. He backed that up with 402 three-pointers in a single season — a record that shattered his own previous record of 286 by a margin so large it felt like science fiction.

  1. First unanimous MVP in NBA history (130–0 first-place votes)
  2. 402 three-pointers — a record he set by breaking his own record by 116
  3. 73 wins with the Warriors — the best regular season record in NBA history
  4. 50-40-90 shooting splits (50% FG, 45% 3PT, 91% FT)
  5. Led the league in scoring at 30.1 PPG

The achievement was truly global: even in Japan, where basketball culture uses the phonetic カリーKarī for Curry, and in Spain where fans call him El MagoThe Magician, the unanimous MVP became frontpage news.

    Frequently Raised Objections — Answered

    Objection: "Curry won because of his teammates."

    Every dynasty requires great teammates. Jordan had Pippen. LeBron had Wade and Bosh. Curry had Klay, Green, and Durant — but he was the engine. The Warriors were a lottery team before Curry's emergence. After he left due to injury in 2019–20, they won 15 games. The system revolved around him. Full stop.

    Objection: "He can't guard anyone defensively."

    Curry has never been an elite defender — true. But he has improved substantially and has shown the ability to guard elite wings in playoff series. His off-ball activity, screen navigation, and steal numbers are underrated. And ultimately: the GOAT debate is not won on defensive stats alone. Scoring, leadership, and cultural impact are equally weighted.

    Objection: "No GOAT goes 6th in a Finals series."

    In 2019, Curry averaged 30.5 PPG in the Finals without Klay Thompson and Kevin Durant — both injured. He was a one-man show against the Raptors and still pushed it to six games. That performance is frequently cited as one of the most impressive Finals performances by a player on the losing side in NBA history.

    Objection: "Jordan never lost in the Finals."

    This is true and a fair point. But Jordan played in an era with a dramatically smaller global talent pool, before widespread international competition, and against defenses that would be illegal under modern rules (hand-checking, physical play). Context matters. Curry's 43%+ from three in contested, high-volume situations has no historical comparison.
